Solution: Night Vision Goggles
Answer: LIGHT A TORCH

Written by Brian Chen, Patrick Xia, Colin Lu, Ben Yang, Jon Schneider, Anderson Wang

Applying the same mechanic to the title, K-Night Vision, combined with the fact that we have squares on a board, suggests the next step: making a knight’s tour. In particular, the above correspondence links each puzzle to a square, and the circle around the board provides a cyclic order that the tour must go through.

The total number of letters in the first words of the answers is 5+4+3+2+4+5+6=29, which is equal to the number of squares on the board without words (36−7=29). This confirms that we’re on the right track and suggests that we’ll spell out these words on the board with knight’s moves, separated by the 7 synonym squares.

In cycle order, the words that we’ll write in the grid are:

We want to find a knight’s tour that visits exactly 3 squares (the length of HEX) in between “Oaf” and “Currency”, exactly 2 squares (the length of HI) between “Currency” and “Drug”, exactly 4 squares (the length of LIFE) between “Drug” and “Brand”, etc. So, if we label the squares of the knight’s tour in order from 1–36, then the following numbers below are fixed:

13 (Brand)30 (Scratched)
8 (Drug)
1 (Oaf)24 (Basketballer)
18 (Poet)5 (Currency)

We could also have put TRIPLE between “Oaf” and “Currency” instead of HEX, then HEX between “Currency” and “Drug”, etc. but this is actually impossible because of parity: if you color the squares alternating black and white like a chessboard, then a knight’s move always connects squares of different colors. “Oaf” and “Currency” are on same-colored squares, so the path must visit an odd number of squares between them, which means it must spell out HEX and not TRIPLE.

This now turns into a sort of logic puzzle where we have to find a knight’s tour that visits the 7 given squares at specific points. There are several strategies that help with this:

  • (probably the most important!) It’s very useful to draw lines between squares that you know are adjacent in the tour—it’s very difficult to keep track of all the constraints otherwise.
  • If a square ever has two lines coming into it, it is “done” and no other lines can use that square.
  • Whenever there’s a square that a knight’s move away from only 2 other un-”done” squares, we can draw in those two lines.
  • In particular, we can immediately draw two lines coming out from each of the 4 corners.
  • The paths between numbers that are closer together (especially 1–5 and 5–8) often have fairly limited possibilities.

Author’s Notes

(Spoiler warning: there are spoilers for a Puzzle Boat 7 meta ahead)

This meta, like all the other metas, was originally written way back in 2019. The first version was written by Jon and also had a knight’s tour, but the mapping of answers to squares used a different knight-inspired mechanic: the answers were phrases like PENT UP, where the first word could be phoentically prefixed with SIR, becoming SIR PENT → SERPENT, and could then be corresponded to a profession in the grid, such as “Zoologist” (our favorite was the answer VALENCE BAND, which would correspond to a spy).

We were quite happy with this mechanic, but unfortunately, while we put our entire hunt on ice due to Mystery Hunt, the intervening Puzzle Boat included a meta with the same prefix-phoentic-”Sir” step; not only that, it was also chess flavored. We debated for a long time what to do with this meta and considered keeping it anyway, but after a lot of discussion about the advantages/disadvantages and brainstorming alternatives, we rewrote it into the current form. We thought this was unfortunately a little less interesting, but in the end was the best option.
