Solution: Inaccessible
Answer: DOCTOR STRANGE

Written by Leo Marchand and Amon Ge

We can’t find our glasses to see this puzzle, but it’s a good thing we have a screen reader to help us! Listening to the screen reader audio, we notice that the puzzle consists of six images of creatures each next to a number. The screen-read flavortext about “the events this year”, the title of the puzzle (Opening Ceremony), and the timing of the hunt all hint at the Olympics, which start on the same day as this hunt. Additionally, both the Olympics and Galactic Puzzle Hunt are definitely taking place in 2020.

If we search for these creatures’ descriptions, we find that they are all mascots — not for the Olympics, but for the Paralympics, which always occur immediately after the Olympics for athletes with disabilities. (This is suggested by the accessibility theme of this actual puzzle, including the title, Inaccessible.)

Once we identify the Paralympic mascots, indexing into their names by their given numbers spells MARVEL. If we carefully listen to the details for each mascot, we notice that each mascot has an extra component (clued by “extra prepared” in the flavor). Combining these items gives the outfit for DOCTOR STRANGE, a Marvel superhero matching the underscore enumeration (6 7) given at the end of the puzzle.

Author’s Notes

We both watch Aurateur, a streamer with text-to-speech (TTS) donations enabled. His chat often spams donations to occupy much of the stream with TTS reading, taking advantage of the voice’s (Amazon Polly’s US English Joanna) many idiosyncracies, including:

  • nonsensical pronunciations, like and (click to play)
  • reading out full emoji names in order to prolong the donation’s TTS length per character, like , , and
  • earthquakes and rhythmic sequences, like , , , and
  • reading repeated as an unusually long silence, which the self-declared Silence Club uses to fight back against the loyalist TTS spam

This puzzle evolved out of this TTS idea after noticing the Olympics’ opening ceremony would be on the same day as the start of GPH, and liking the idea of using a screen reader to read a puzzle we couldn’t see. The underscore enumeration at the end of the puzzle is music to our ears. We used ttsmp3.com to generate Joanna’s audio for the puzzle.
