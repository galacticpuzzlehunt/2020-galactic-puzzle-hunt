Solution: The Seasons Change
Answer: MISO SOUP

Written by Nathan Pinsker

The puzzle provides us with 36 Japanese courses corresponding to four different restaurants and asks that we order them. The title and flavor text heavily reference seasonality, or shun, and a little research may uncover kaiseki ryori, an "ultra-seasonal" style of Japanese cuisine most strongly associated with Kyoto. This yields the insight that all four restaurants are kaiseki restaurants.

Since 36 is divisible by 4, this suggests grouping the courses into four groups of nine. The puzzle now becomes one of determining which courses are served, which order they were served in, and the course that each dish corresponds to.

Some dishes have explicit clues as to their position or season, but it seems like we don't have enough information to solve the puzzle. However, the ingredients themselves serve as clues: because they're served "at their very best" — that is, during the season that they are at their peak — this greatly restricts which meals they can belong to. For example, ginkgo nuts and persimmons are traditionally autumn foods. Here is a table of all lesser-known ingredients and the seasons they're most commonly associated with.

There's inherently some ambiguity to this table, and it is unlikely that solvers' research will agree with the table in every entry. To help with the puzzle's inherent ambiguity, there is substantial additional cross-checking in the food descriptions. We present one possible path to the solution now that uses a minority of the table's information; note there are numerous other potential paths that make use of different parts of the table.

The kaiseki course list varies by restaurant, but virtually all include sakizuke (appetizer) at the beginning and mizumono (dessert) at the end. The missing sakizuke and hassun dishes can be determined by process of elimination, which reveals that the restaurant in spring is the one that serves completely vegetarian fare.

Determining the remaining courses is a bit trickier. One dish mentions a "soup course" and one dish explicitly mentions the yakimono course, although we can't pin down either's position in the meal yet. Two explicit mentions of sashimi (and two additional sashimi dishes) suggest a mukozuke (sashimi) course. One of the sashimi dishes mentions a plate "nowhere near as pretty", contrasting it against the ebi sushi "served on a beautiful plate", suggesting that sashimi is the third course. An "onigiri of sea bream" dish is served directly before dessert, which suggests that the penultimate course is gohan (rice and seasonal ingredients).

Since the dandelion and cherry blossom chazuke was "following the soup course", it follows that the third-to-last course is a soup course such as suimono. The dish of "bracken and ostrich fern, simmered and served alongside tofu" comes immediately after another tofu dish, which by process of elimination means directly after the yuba sashimi in spring. Another mention of "simmered" and one of "braised" also suggest that this course is the takiawase (simmered) course. Finally, one course of diced ginger and ume mentions that the course "is usually vegetables", suggesting the final unknown course is su-zakana (acidic vegetable palate cleanser).

Reading the first letters of all 36 dishes (in order of when the narrator ate them) gives the message COMBINE DASHI AND SOYBEAN PASTE TO GET WHAT. "Soybean paste" refers to miso, a seasoning core to Japanese cuisine; combining it with dashi gives MISO SOUP, the answer to the puzzle.

Author’s Notes

I wrote this puzzle back in 2019, before we won the Mystery Hunt and subsequently shifted our attention towards preparing that. I've often tended towards interactive puzzles with a focus on production and humor, so this was a good opportunity to try a more "traditional" puzzle type with more tightly integrated theming.

Thanks to the book Kaiseki: The Exquisite Cuisine of Kyoto's Kikunoi Restaurant for assistance with dish ideas. I definitely had to shove an inappropriate dish in here and there for the sake of the extraction, but hopefully the courses ended up mostly believable.
