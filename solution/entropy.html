Guess Hint Puzzle

Solution: Entropy
Answer: HI HAT

Written by Dai Yang

The puzzle contains a collection of 9 poems. The poems have distinctive styles and rhyme schemes, but all of them are about science in some way, in particular about physics.

The first step is to identify poets, as suggested by the flavor text: “I like to pay tribute to famous poets within the text of my poetry”. Each poem corresponds to a notable poem or style of poems by a famous poet. This can be done by looking at the rhyme scheme, meter, choice of words, line length, and in some cases punctuation as well.

The next step is to look for the poets’ names in the text of each poem. It turns out that each poem contains a sequence of words that is an anagram of the author’s first name + 1 letter, followed by a sequence of words that is an anagram of the author’s last name + 1 letter. This is clued by both the flavor text and the puzzle title, “Entropy”, which is an anagram of “Poetry” plus one letter. This allows us to extract two letters from each poem.

When read in given order, the pairs of letters spell out USEMORINAABBAPOEMS. An AABBA poem is a limerick, so the cluephrase refers to a collection of physics limericks written by Harvard professor David Morin.

The next step is to find commonalities between the poems in the puzzle and the limericks. If we match up the poems and the limericks by theme, we can notice some overlap in the line-ending words. For example, in Morin’s limerick about bad induction, the three line-end words with the A rhyme pattern are ASSIGN, DEFINE, and, NINE and the two line-end words with the B rhyme pattern are INSTRUCTION and INDUCTION. In the puzzle, the poem about bad induction also has two of these A rhyme pattern words and one of these B rhyme pattern words at line-ends:

Generalizing on deduction, let us study the induction, This trick needs no introduction, quoth our teacher Theodore. Take a horse, a black equine, monochrome so we define, A base case for our logic line, learn this well or out the door. A base case guaranteeing claims each follow from the one before. Thusly argued Theodore.

Groups of horses we combine, from two to three or eight to nine. Even thousands pose no sign of trouble for our metaphor. This leads us to epiphany: every horse is ebony. To contradict is felony, remarked our teacher Theodore. At this absurdity we quaked, in unison we did implore: Teach induction nevermore!

This gives us one missing A word and one missing B word from each poem, which fit exactly into the blanks immediately below the poem, with the A word being first. Each set of blanks has one blank labelled with a number from 1 to 18, so by finding all the missing words, we can obtain an 18-letter phrase.
